TITLE: Greenwich Millennium Village v Essex Services Group and Others

PUBLICATION: Keating Chambers Case Summary

DATE: 25 October 2013



CITATION: [2014] EWHC 1099 (TCC), [2014] TCLR 4, [2013] 3059 (TCC), 151 Con LR 1



FACTS:

Greenwich Millennium Village Ltd ("GMV") brought subrogated claims against several defendants in respect of catastrophic flooding at a residential development owned by GMV. The flooding was the result of various failures in construction and caused £4.75 million worth of damage. The complexity and ineptitude of the contractual arrangements involved were such as to provoke Coulson J's assessment of the failures as "death by sub-contracting."



ISSUES:

The causes of two separate but related failures in the building's cold water system, and which parties were responsible.



JUDGMENT (Coulson J):



FIRST FAILURE:

- Caused by both (1) presence of metallic debris in the water system, and (2) over-tightening of a nut forming part of that system

- Essex Services were responsible for both causes

- Liability passed down the contractual chain via HSE to D G Robson Mechanical Services Limited



SECOND FAILURE:

- Two independent causes: (1) installation of a non-return valve where none belonged, and (2) leaving of an isolation valve in the closed position

- Either cause alone would have prevented the critical Anti Surge Valve from operating

- Judge departed from the "but for" test of causation — both independent causes were effective causes

- Both Essex Service and Hoare Lea responsible; liability apportioned 60% / 40% respectively

- Essex Service's liability passed down via HSE to D G Robson Mechanical Services



COUNSEL:

- Piers Stansfield QC (now KC) appeared on behalf of the Claimant

- Simon Hargreaves QC (now KC) appeared on behalf of the Respondent/Third Party

COURT OF APPEAL DECISION (2014):

CITATION: [2014] EWCA Civ (Crown Office Chambers reference)

Issue: Scope of indemnity where an indemnitee had itself failed to detect defective work on inspection

Held: An indemnity could still operate despite the indemnitee's "passive" failure to spot defects on inspection. Considered an important construction-law authority with widespread implications for the industry.
