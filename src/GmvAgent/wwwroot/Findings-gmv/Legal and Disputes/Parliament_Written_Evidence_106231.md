Background and Introduction

GMV Management Ltd (“GMVML”) is a Residents’ Management Company incorporated for the sole purpose of managing Greenwich Millennium Village – Phases 1 & 2 (“the Village”). It is a non-trading company with no assets nor income of its own. The Direct ors of GMVML are volunteer l easeholders who are as equally affected by this scandal as all other leaseholders at the Village.All costs i ncurred by GMVML, in fulfilling its contractual and statutory obligations, are recovered as variable service charges from leaseholders.

The Villag e forms part of Greenwich Millennium Village, the freehold of which is owned by the Greater London Authority (“GLA”).

The Village is a mixed-use (predominantly residential) development comprising 1,097 residential dwellings and 11 small commercial properti es. The residential dwellings comprise of 10 high-rise (above 18m) apartment blocks and 6 low-rise (below 11m) apartment blocks together with a small number of houses.

The Village was constructed in a number of phases around 20 years ago,as a joint venture between Countryside and Taylor Wimpey, in association with the Mayor of London. The joint venture company (Greenwich Millennium Village Limited) is still operating and is currently developing additional phases which do not constitute part of the Vi llage managed by GMVML.

Following the publication of the consolidated guidanc e of The Government’s Expert Advisory Panel on Fire Safety on 20 th January 2020, GMVML commissioned intrusive surveys o f the External Wall Systems (“EWS”). All 10 high-rise buildi ngs were deemed to require EWS remediation works to ensure they are of limited combustibility and can thereafter by certified as ‘safe’. The remediation works identified broadly comprise of the removal and replacement of cladding and the installation of su itable fire breaks in locations where existing fire breaks are either missing or inadequate.

All 10 high-rise buildings have received initial eligibility and funding confirmation from the Building Safety Fund (“BSF”) for removal and replacement of cladding to the EWS. We await confirmation from BSF on the level of funds to be made available to enable a works contract to be executed and remediation works to commence.

In line with the consolidated guidance, i ntrusive surveys were also commissioned on the low-rise buildings. The Fire Engineers identified a number of issues on these buildings and were unable to issue EWS1 certificates c ertify ing all of the low-rise buildings as being safe. The continu ed ambiguity in respect of fire safety certification for b uildings below 11m is causing enormous stress to the Board and our leaseholders.

Evidence

*   The Government’s announcements on 10 January 2022 are welcome but they are far from solving the problems for leaseholders. 

*   We particularly welcome the intention to put pressure on developers and materials’ manufacturers, who caused the problems. That is the right thing to do, but if the Government’s actions do not have any ‘teeth’ behind them, there is a risk that they are just hollow words.

*   We also welcome the inte ntion to ensure a risk based proportionate approach to building safety,following the withdrawal of the c onsolidated guidance. Many of the present problems arise from the implications of the guidance and other statements, and the inferences drawn from them, that all buildings with cladding were subject to the same very high level of risk.

*   The key exclusion from all of the announcements so far is the absence of support for non-cladding deficiencies. This still has a big impact upon leaseholders even if cladding remediation works are (eventually) fully funded by the BSF.Remediation of non-cladding deficiencies could result in costs running into thousands of pounds for leaseholders. As a Resident Management Company, we will be left with the problem of seeking payment from leaseholders, some of whom will choose not to pay for this scandal and others who simply can not afford to pay.

*   Even if / when BSF funding is available, a s a Residents’ Management Company with no resources, we ar e unable to execute a contract to commence (non-cladding) reme diation works until funds are available to pay for those works in their entirety. Difficulties in recovering costs in advance from leaseholders could result in lengthy (even indefinite) delays i n undertaking the necessary remediation works. 

*   The lack of funding to remediate non-cladding deficiencies is also likely to result in lengthy (even indefinite) delays in undertaking the removal and replacement of cladding. It is simply not possible to remediate one element of an EWS without taking a holistic approach to the whole wall system and rectifying all deficiencies at the same time. The BSF w ill fail to ensure the removal and replacement of non-compliant cladding systems if funds are not (somehow) made available to simultaneously undertake the remaining remediation works.

*   The Secretary of State might acknowledge that the BSF process is taking a long time, but we need to see action being taken to expedite this process and prescriptive timelines or ‘service levels’ defined, which will give R esidents’ M anagement C ompanies some clarity on when we can execute a building contra ct and get started on r emediation.
*   Many of our leaseholders are effectively trapped in their properties and are not able to move on or sell. This is especially important and distressing for those with growing families or others who need to move away from the area for either personal or professional reasons.
*   We need the arrangement with industry to cover all safety related defects, not just cladding.
*   The proposal to extend the defects period out to 30 years is welcome, but it needs to be retrospective.The Government also needs to im pose / implement the enforcement of retrospective remediation of deficiencies. Without any Government enforcement, it will still be down to leaseholders to take legal action against developers (where they still exist). In practice, develop ers will deny that identified deficiencies are ‘defect s that did not comply with Building Regulations at the time of construction’. The outcome of l egal ac tion to seek remediation of identified deficiencies is far from certain, will take years and is prohi bitively expensive. As a Residents’ Management Company, we have no resources to commence any such legal action and would be in danger of breaching our fiduciary duties if we commit our leaseholders’ service charge monies to such an uncertain and expensive process. As Board Directors we are fearful of breaching our statutory duty of trust in respect of the service charge monies we collect and hold on behalf of our leaseholders.
*   W e welcome the announcement that more blocks are being promised Government suppor t. Whilst the announcement s provide positive new s for leaseholder in blocks 11-18m, it is unclear what the implication will be for those leaseholders and residents within blocks over 18m, who remain stuck within the BSF process. If more blocks are being included in the scope for support from the government, will this result i n the same amount of government resource being spread even more thinly and therefore take even longer for the BSF a pplication s to progress?
*   We are also concerned about the impact upon the construction sector of wider Government support and the subsequent impact upon the timescales and costs of remediation works. Higher demand will be generated f or the same limited skil ls and resources of companies and products to actually complete the remediation. This is likely to have significant inflationary pressure on the costs of works. We need assurance from the G overnment that any funding takes account of this inflationary pre ssure, that has been caused as a direct consequence of the G overnment’s approach.It is unacceptable for a successful BSF application to leave a shortfall, that leaseholders have to cover. This will result in direct costs to leaseholders, that many will not be able to pay and will lead to delays in executing contracts and commencing remediation works. The issue is particularly troubling and difficult for Residents’ Management Companies (like ourselves) with no resources. 
*   We welcome the intention to ensur e a risk based proportionate approach to building safety, but we are conce rned about the continued ambiguity in relation to remediation works for low-rise buildings below 11m. It is still not clear if the removal of the consolidated guidance has increased the lending appetite of mortgage lenders. This is particularly confusing in respect of low-rise buildings where compliance with the consolidated guidance ide ntified deficiencies. It is unnecessarily stressful for leaseholders who do not know if remediation works are (stil l) required, who is responsible for undertaking those remediation works, where funding is going to come from and whether they can afford to continue to own their home. The inability to sell or refinance has created an unnecess arily stressful situation for leaseholders who are trapped within these properties.

Conclusions

In conclusion, we welcome the Government’s announcements. We remain concerned, however, that:

*   The BSF approval process needs to be significantly spe d up
*   Fundin g is required for non-cladding remediations
*   T he Government needs to take a pro-active approach to the enfor cement of remediation of historic defects against developers
*   Government financial support needs to reflect the supply and inflationary pressures it w ill create on the construction and materials sectors
*   The continued ambiguity in relation to low-rise buildings (below 11m) needs to be resolved by government and, in particular, with mortgage lenders

The Secretary of State’s announcements de monstrate a change in rhetoric from ‘building owners should pay’ to ‘developers should pay’. Both of these rhetorics, however, fail to recognise any appreciation of the impossible situation faced by Residents’ Management Companies like ourselves. The Gover nment needs to implement a ‘holistic’ approach to ensure that funding is made available, in advance, for ALL necessary remediation works. The Government needs to take a proactive approach to enforcement and / or recovery of funds from those considered resp onsible for the deficiencies.

Secretary of State Announcement on 1 4 February

We note the Secretary of State’s further announcement on 1 4 February and welcome the direction of travel to make developers pay to remediate all historic deficiencies in building s up to 30 years old. Our response above, howe ver, concentrates on the questions posed by the Committee in its call for evidence prior to the latest announcement by the Secretary of State.

We note, however, that the further proposals include a cap on the level of service charges recovered from leaseholders in certain circumstances. We wi sh to reiterate our points above that proposals which continue to impose financial and legal demands—even if financially capped—on leaseholders could cause the same concern s and delays that the proposals aim to avoid.
